Math/science knowledge trade-off?

Country Math score Science score

The intuition might suggest that is a negative correlation between math and science performance. After all, there are finite resources (e.g. time of the curriculum used in teaching each subject) that the institutions might use in order to improve the results of the fourth graders in each of this areas of knowledge.

Nevertheless, this hypothesis is not supported by data. As seen in the above scatterplot, the correlation is positive even to the eye. The implications of this are very interesting, because it suggest that there is not a trade-off between math and sciences. Developing abilities in one area does not exclude improving abilities in the other. A good score in the math test does not imply a bad score in the science test. And it is a very interesting conclusion because it supports the idea that scientific and math thinking are not that different at all. In other words, scientific thinking is as important to mathematical thinking as mathematical thinking is to scientific thinking. Improving the performance of the students in one of these areas will also increase the performance in the other. After all, mathematics is an exact science, and although it might be more quantitative than other sciences, the scientific method could be applied to either.

Source of dataset: National Center of Educational Statistics
